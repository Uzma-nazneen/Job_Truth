The Real-World Evaluation Trap (Class Imbalance)

The Accuracy Paradox: In the original job posting corpus, only 4.84% (866 out of 17,880) of listings are fraudulent. A naive model that classifies every job as "legitimate" would achieve an accuracy of 95.16%. Thus, standard validation accuracy is highly optimistic.

Evaluating Imbalance Performance: In a real-world scenario (95% Legitimate vs 5% Fraudulent), the Random Forest model achieves a high sensitivity (recall) of 97.3%, catching almost all scams. However, due to the sheer size of the legitimate pool, the model generates false flags.

True Positive Predictive Value (Precision): When deployed in production on the full imbalanced corpus:

  • True Positives caught: ~842 fake job posts.
  • False Positives (legitimate jobs flagged as fake): ~459 flags.
  • Actual Precision: ~64.7% (only 2 out of 3 flagged jobs are actually fraudulent).

This highlights why standard accuracy metrics are overly optimistic, and why practical evaluations must prioritize precision-recall trade-offs over raw accuracy.

Key Insights & Methodology

1. The Company Profile Heuristic: Statistical analysis shows that one of the strongest features in identifying fraud is the presence of a detailed company_profile. Out of the fraudulent listings analysed in the Jupyter dataset, over 80% did not include any background description of the hiring firm.

2. Spam Keyword Density: Text modeling confirms fraudulent job postings depend heavily on templated promotional phrases. The system flags words like "immediate wire transfer", "envelope stuffing", and "package handler" which show extremely high conditional probabilities in the Multinomial Naive Bayes model.

3. The Logistical Bias Check: Over 75% of fraudulent job postings in the database did not offer screening questions (has_questions = 0). This serves as a vital structural signal because legitimate employers tend to incorporate questionnaires to parse large applicant queues.

4. Description Length Indicators: Real job descriptions follow normal distributions in length, usually outlining detailed technical tasks and company operations. Fraudulent jobs display high kurtosis, tending to be either extremely brief (under 500 characters) or excessively filled with formatting spam.
